# Holistic Wealth Management for Wealthy Immigrants in California
## A Complete Roadmap: Licenses, Knowledge, Firm Setup, and Client Acquisition

*Research compiled: April 2026*

---

## OVERVIEW

This roadmap covers everything needed to become a licensed holistic wealth consultant
serving high-net-worth (HNW) immigrant clients in California, including opening your
own independent RIA firm. The roadmap is organized into three phases:

- **Phase 1 (Months 1–18):** Education, Licensing, and Designation
- **Phase 2 (Months 12–24):** Firm Formation and Registration
- **Phase 3 (Months 18–36+):** Client Acquisition and Practice Growth

## PART 1: LICENSE AND CREDENTIAL REQUIREMENTS

### 1.1 The Baseline: Series 65 (Required)

The **Series 65 (Uniform Investment Adviser Law Examination)** is the foundational
license for operating as an investment adviser representative (IAR) in California.

- **Administered by:** NASAA / FINRA
- **Exam format:** 130 scored + 10 unscored questions; need 92/130 to pass
- **Fee:** $187
- **Sponsor required:** No — you can self-register, making it ideal for starting your own RIA
- **Study window:** 120 days after registration
- **Topics covered:** Laws, regulations, ethics, investment vehicles, portfolio management,
  economics, client management

**Alternative path:** Pass both the Series 7 (requires a FINRA-member firm sponsor) +
Series 66. This path is harder to self-start but enables brokerage services too.

**Exam waivers** — you can skip Series 65 if you already hold one of these in good standing:
- CFP® (Certified Financial Planner)
- CFA® (Chartered Financial Analyst)
- CIC (Chartered Investment Counselor)
- ChFC® (Chartered Financial Consultant)
- PFS (Personal Financial Specialist — CPA-based)

### 1.2 Professional Designations (Strongly Recommended)

These designations go beyond the legal minimum and signal expertise to sophisticated clients.

#### CFP® — Certified Financial Planner (Most Important)
The gold-standard credential for holistic financial planning.

- **Topics:** Investment planning, tax planning, retirement planning, estate planning,
  insurance, employee benefits
- **Requirements:**
  - Bachelor's degree from accredited US university
  - Complete CFP Board-approved education program (~12–18 months if self-paced)
  - 6,000 hours qualifying work experience (or 4,000 apprenticeship hours)
  - Pass CFP exam (170 questions, two 3-hour sessions, one day)
  - Adhere to CFP Board ethics standards
- **Cost:** ~$4,500–$6,000 coursework + $925 exam (standard) + $200 application + $455/yr membership
- **Exam offered:** March, July, November (3 times/year)
- **Why it matters:** Waives Series 65; most recognized credential with wealthy clients;
  covers the holistic planning model you want to deliver
- **Issuer:** CFP Board (cfp.net)

#### CPWA® — Certified Private Wealth Advisor (Highly Relevant for HNW Clients)
Advanced designation focused specifically on ultra-high-net-worth (UHNW) clients.

- **Topics:** Advanced estate planning, tax strategies, behavioral finance, family dynamics,
  legacy planning, wealth transfer, alternative investments
- **Requirements:**
  - Bachelor's degree + CFP/CFA/CIMA/CPA (prerequisite — get CFP first)
  - 5 years financial services experience
  - Complete Yale SOM or University of Chicago executive education program
  - Pass CPWA exam
- **Why it matters:** Directly signals expertise with HNW families — exactly your target market
- **Issuer:** Investments & Wealth Institute

#### CPA — Certified Public Accountant (Optional but Powerful)
If you want to offer integrated tax services, becoming a CPA adds tremendous value.

- **Requirements:** 150 semester credit hours of education, pass 4-part CPA exam, 1 year
  experience under licensed CPA
- **Why it matters:** Wealthy immigrants need complex multi-jurisdiction tax planning.
  A CPA-CFP combination is extremely rare and valuable in this niche.
- **California Regulator:** California Board of Accountancy

#### EA — Enrolled Agent (Practical Alternative to CPA for Tax Focus)
Federally-licensed tax practitioners authorized to represent taxpayers before the IRS.

- **Requirements:** Pass 3-part Special Enrollment Examination (SEE) — no degree required
- **Cost:** ~$600 total exam fees; achievable in 6–12 months
- **Why it matters:** Especially relevant for FBAR/FATCA filings and IRS representation
  for foreign-income issues. Faster to obtain than CPA.

#### JD — Juris Doctor (Optional, for Estate Planning Focus)
An active California law license enables you to draft trusts, wills, and legal structures.

- **Time:** 3 years law school + bar exam
- **Why it matters:** Estate planning for non-domiciliaries involves complex legal structures
  (ILITs, QPRTs, foreign trusts). Having JD lets you do in-house what others must
  outsource to attorneys.
- **Note:** Most RIA practitioners collaborate with estate attorneys rather than getting a JD.
  Consider a strong referral network with estate planning attorneys instead.
  An **LLM in Taxation** (1 year post-JD) is the fastest route to tax law credibility
  if you already have a law degree.

#### Additional Specialty Designations for Your Niche

| Designation | Issuer | Focus | Relevance |
|-------------|--------|-------|-----------|
| **TEP** (Trust and Estate Practitioner) | STEP | International estate/trust planning | Very High for cross-border |
| **AEP** (Accredited Estate Planner) | NAEPC | Estate planning | High |
| **CIMA** (Certified Investment Management Analyst) | Investments & Wealth Institute | Investment consulting | High |
| **CFA** (Chartered Financial Analyst) | CFA Institute | Investment management | High (waives Series 65) |
| **CAIA** (Chartered Alternative Investment Analyst) | CAIA Association | Alternatives | Medium-high |

**Most Valuable Combo for Your Niche:** CFP + EA (or CPA) + Series 65 →
then CPWA + TEP as your practice matures.

## PART 2: CRITICAL KNOWLEDGE AREAS FOR SERVING IMMIGRANT HNW CLIENTS

This is your real competitive advantage. Most advisors don't understand this intersection.

### 2.1 US Tax Law for Non-Citizens and New Immigrants

**Key concepts to master:**

- **Resident vs. Non-Resident Alien:** Determined by Green Card test or Substantial
  Presence Test (183-day rule). Tax obligations differ dramatically.
- **Dual-Status returns:** Clients who become residents mid-year file both resident
  and non-resident portions. Complex first-year planning opportunities exist here.
- **First-year election:** Married to a US citizen? Can elect full-year resident status
  for joint filing benefits — but subjects worldwide income to US tax.
- **Worldwide income taxation:** Once a green card holder or resident alien, ALL
  worldwide income is taxable in the US — clients often don't know this.
- **Pre-immigration tax planning:** Extremely high-value service — helping clients
  restructure assets BEFORE they get their green card can save millions in taxes.
  - Accelerate income/capital gains before US tax residency begins
  - Establish foreign trusts before becoming a US resident (deferrals possible)
  - Recognize gains on appreciated foreign assets pre-immigration (step-up in basis)
  - Plan timing of asset recognition (bonuses, business distributions)
  - Most immigrants and their former advisors miss this window — this is your opportunity
- **Foreign Tax Credit (Form 1116):** Offsets double taxation for income taxed abroad.
- **Foreign Earned Income Exclusion (FEIE):** For clients who still work abroad.
- **PFICs (Passive Foreign Investment Companies):** Most foreign mutual funds qualify as
  PFICs; subject to punitive US tax treatment. Identifying and restructuring before
  tax residency begins is a high-value planning service.

**Key IRS forms to know:** 1040-NR, 1040 (dual-status), Form 1116, Form 2555, Form 8833

### 2.2 Foreign Asset Reporting (FBAR & FATCA)

Your clients will almost certainly have foreign accounts and assets — compliance is non-optional.

**FBAR (FinCEN Form 114):**
- Required when: Aggregate value of foreign financial accounts exceeds $10,000 at ANY
  point during the year
- Filed with FinCEN (NOT the IRS), via BSA E-Filing System
- Deadline: April 15, automatic extension to October 15
- Penalties for negligent non-filing: up to $10,000/year
- Penalties for willful non-filing: Up to $100,000 per violation or 50% of account value

**FATCA (Form 8938, filed with tax return):**
- Required when: Foreign financial assets exceed $50,000 (US resident, filing single at
  year end) or $200,000–$300,000 for expats abroad
- Broader than FBAR — covers not just bank accounts but foreign stocks, partnerships,
  pension plans, foreign trusts
- Filed WITH Form 1040 (not separately)
- Penalties: $10,000–$50,000 for non-filing; criminal exposure for willful violations

**Other critical foreign reporting forms:**
- **Form 3520:** Foreign gifts, inheritances, and transactions with foreign trusts
  (very common for wealthy immigrants receiving family wealth transfers)
- **Form 5471:** Ownership in foreign corporations
- **Form 8621:** Passive Foreign Investment Companies (PFICs) — high-complexity, punitive
  default tax treatment if not handled correctly
- **Form 8858:** Foreign Disregarded Entities
- **Form 926:** Transfers to foreign corporations

**Note:** Many wealthy immigrants arrive with foreign mutual funds that qualify as PFICs.
These carry extremely punitive US tax treatment. Identifying and restructuring them
before tax residency begins is one of the most valuable services you can offer.

### 2.3 Estate Planning for Non-US Domiciliaries

**The $60,000 trap:** Non-US domiciled individuals only get a $60,000 estate tax
exemption (vs. ~$15 million for US citizens/domiciliaries starting January 1, 2026).
Assets above $60,000 in US-situs property are taxed at up to 40%.

**US situs assets include:** US real estate, US stocks/bonds, US bank accounts (for
some types), US-located personal property.

**Domicile determination is complex:**
- Domicile for estate tax ≠ income tax residency
- Intent + presence test; even visa type matters (e.g., G-4 visa holders may be
  US-domiciled for estate tax purposes even if non-resident for income tax)
- Must analyze carefully for every client

**Estate and gift tax treaties:** The US has estate/gift tax treaties with ~16 countries
(Australia, Canada, France, Germany, Japan, Netherlands, UK, etc.) that can increase
exemptions and reduce double taxation. Know which treaties apply to your clients' home countries.

**Key planning strategies:**
- **QDOT (Qualified Domestic Trust):** Non-citizen surviving spouses cannot access the
  unlimited marital deduction — QDOTs allow tax deferral for non-citizen spouses
- **ILIT (Irrevocable Life Insurance Trust):** Life insurance in an ILIT passes
  estate-tax-free — powerful tool for non-domiciliaries
- **Foreign holding companies:** Owning US real estate through a foreign corporation
  can convert US-situs to non-situs property (complex — requires attorney)
- **Non-US life insurance policies:** Can fund estate taxes without becoming part of the
  US taxable estate
- **Gifting strategies:** Annual exclusion gifts ($18,000/year general; $185,000+/year
  to non-citizen spouses); lifetime exemption planning

### 2.4 Investment Management

- Portfolio construction for multi-currency, multi-jurisdiction families
- Alternative investments (private equity, real estate, hedge funds) — common HNW allocations
- Understanding PFIC rules and how to avoid them in portfolio construction
- Tax-efficient investing (tax-loss harvesting, asset location across accounts)
- Understanding concentrated positions (common with entrepreneur immigrants who sold
  businesses abroad before moving)
- Currency risk management
- Foreign pension plan treatment — may not receive same US tax deferral as 401(k)

### 2.5 Immigration-Wealth Intersections

**EB-5 Investor Visa:**
- Requires minimum $800,000–$1,050,000 investment in a US commercial enterprise
  creating 10+ jobs
- Advisors who understand EB-5 can facilitate introductions and plan around the capital lock-up
- Coordinate with immigration attorneys on EB-5 Regional Center investments

**Pre-immigration planning (Highest-value service):**
The window between visa approval and actual green card receipt is critical. Services:
- Restructure foreign investment portfolios to eliminate or reduce PFIC exposure
- Set up foreign trust structures before US tax residency begins
- Plan the timing of asset recognition (gains, distributions) before worldwide income kicks in
- Step-up in basis planning

**Exit planning (Expatriation):**
Some clients may eventually expatriate. Understand the "exit tax" rules for long-term
residents (8 of last 15 years as LPR) — Code Section 877A.

**Green card timing strategy:**
When a client obtains a green card, US tax residency starts the first day in the calendar
year they are present as a lawful permanent resident. This timing matters enormously
for tax planning.

**Naturalization considerations:**
US citizenship eliminates the QDOT requirement for surviving spouses, changes estate
planning calculus, and removes the non-domiciliary estate tax disadvantage.

**Remittance and wire transfers:**
Understand Bank Secrecy Act reporting requirements (Form 8300 for cash over $10,000;
international wire reporting). Many HNW immigrant clients support family abroad — gift
tax rules apply to large transfers.

## PART 3: REGULATORY FRAMEWORK

### 3.1 Who Regulates You?

| AUM Level | Regulator | Registration |
|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| Under $25M | California DFPI (must register if 5+ CA clients) | State only |
| $25M–$100M | California DFPI | State only |
| $100M–$110M | Choice: DFPI or SEC (buffer zone) | State or SEC |
| Over $110M | SEC | Federal (with notice filings to states) |
| 15+ states | SEC exemption available | Federal |

**Starting out:** You will register with California DFPI. You switch to SEC registration
when you cross $100M AUM (required at $110M).

### 3.2 Key Registrations and Forms

**Form ADV (Investment Adviser Registration Form):**
- Part 1: Business information, ownership, disciplinary history — filed with IARD
- Part 2A: Firm brochure (plain-language description of services, fees, conflicts)
- Part 2B: Brochure supplement (about individual advisers)
- Must be updated annually within 90 days of fiscal year end; material changes require prompt amendment

**Form U4:** Individual IAR registration — filed for each adviser at the firm

**IARD (Investment Adviser Registration Depository):**
The electronic filing system for all advisers. Administered by FINRA on behalf of states.

### 3.3 California-Specific Financial Requirements

| Situation | Minimum Net Worth |
|-----------|------------------|
| No custody, no prepayment | $10,000 |
| Discretionary authority (no custody) | $10,000 |
| Custody of client assets | $35,000 |
| Prepayment > $500 for 6+ months in advance | Positive net worth |

Must file annual balance sheet with DFPI.

**IAR Annual CE (as of 2024):**
12 hours annually: 6 hours products/practices + 6 hours ethics/regulatory

**IAR filing fee (effective July 1, 2025):** $50 per IAR per year

## PART 4: OPENING YOUR OWN RIA FIRM IN CALIFORNIA

### 4.1 Entity Formation

1. Choose entity type:
   - **LLC:** Most common. Flexible, pass-through taxation, simple to manage.
   - **S-Corp:** Better when annual profit exceeds ~$80,000–$100,000 (reduces
     self-employment taxes on distributions above a reasonable salary)
2. File Articles of Organization with California Secretary of State ($70 fee)
3. Get EIN from IRS (free, online)
4. Open a business bank account
5. Register with California FTB for state taxes ($800/year minimum franchise tax for LLC)

### 4.2 Registration Process (Step-by-Step)

**Step 1: Pass Series 65** (or hold qualifying designation)

**Step 2: Create IARD Account**
- Visit iard.com
- Pay initial IARD system processing fee

**Step 3: Complete Form ADV**
- Part 1: Business, ownership, services, fees, custody
- Part 2A: Write your firm brochure
- Part 2B: Your individual brochure supplement
- File through IARD

**Step 4: File with California DFPI**
- Pay California firm registration fee (verify current amount at dfpi.ca.gov)
- IAR filing fee: $50/IAR (effective July 1, 2025)
- Submit California Verification Form + annual balance sheet + financial requirements worksheet
- DFPI review takes approximately 30–45 days

**Step 5: File Form U4**
- Register yourself as an IAR of your new firm
- Background check authorized

**Step 6: Meet financial requirements**
- Maintain minimum net worth ($10,000 or $35,000 depending on custody)
- Keep records showing ongoing compliance

### 4.3 Compliance Infrastructure

**Compliance policies and procedures manual** — Required. Must cover:
- Code of Ethics
- Personal trading / insider trading policies
- Anti-money laundering (AML) awareness
- Conflicts of interest disclosure
- Fee billing procedures
- Recordkeeping policy
- Cybersecurity policy
- Business continuity / disaster recovery plan
- Proxy voting policy (if you vote proxies for clients)

**Chief Compliance Officer (CCO):** You will be your own CCO initially. Annual review
of compliance program is required.

**Recordkeeping:** 5 years minimum (3 years in readily accessible location). Keep:
- Client agreements
- Correspondence and emails
- Trade records
- Financial statements
- Form ADV and amendments

**Annual obligations:**
- File ADV annual amendment within 90 days of fiscal year end
- Pay IAR renewal fees ($50/IAR)
- Complete 12 hours IAR CE annually
- Annual compliance program review
- File annual balance sheet with DFPI

### 4.4 E&O Insurance

Errors & Omissions insurance is required by most custodians and is essential protection.

- **Typical cost:** $2,500–$4,000/year for $1M coverage
- **Providers:** NAPLIA, Travelers, Chubb, Philadelphia Insurance, CalSurance (RIA-specific)
- Obtain before opening to clients
- Also consider: General Liability + Cyber Liability insurance

### 4.5 Custodian Selection

You cannot hold client assets yourself without triggering custody requirements. Use a
qualified custodian:

| Custodian | Minimum AUM | Notes |
|-----------|-------------|-------|
| **Charles Schwab Advisor Services** | None | Best for startups; excellent platform |
| **Fidelity Institutional** | None | Strong technology; good support |
| **Pershing (BNY Mellon)** | ~$100M+ | For larger firms |
| **Interactive Brokers** | None | Good for international securities and foreign account holders |

**For your clients (international focus):** Interactive Brokers is especially worth
considering — it supports international account holders, foreign securities, and
multi-currency accounts. Schwab is the easiest to start with. Consider using both.

### 4.6 Technology Stack

- **Portfolio management software:** Orion, Tamarac, Altruist (affordable for startups)
- **CRM:** Salesforce Financial Services Cloud, Redtail, Wealthbox
- **Financial planning software:** eMoney, MoneyGuidePro, RightCapital
- **Tax planning:** Holistiplan (excellent for CFPs; integrates tax analysis into financial plans)
- **Compliance management:** MyComplianceOffice, ComplySci, MyRIALawyer
- **Video conferencing:** Zoom + virtual office for international clients
- **Document management:** DocuSign, Box, ShareFile

## PART 5: CLIENT ACQUISITION FOR WEALTHY IMMIGRANTS

### 5.1 Your Niche Advantage

Most advisors do not understand cross-border tax, FBAR/FATCA, pre-immigration planning,
or estate planning for non-domiciliaries. Your niche expertise is a genuine competitive moat.
Clients will pay premium fees for an adviser who speaks their language (literally and
figuratively) and understands their unique situation.

### 5.2 Target Client Profile

- Recent immigrants (1–10 years in the US) with ongoing foreign assets
- HNW tech executives (H-1B → Green Card pipeline — Silicon Valley/Bay Area)
- Chinese, Taiwanese, Indian, Korean, Iranian, Israeli immigrants (large CA communities)
- EB-5 investors who need ongoing wealth management post-visa approval
- International entrepreneurs who have sold businesses abroad
- Multi-generational families with cross-border estate planning needs
- Pre-immigration clients (referred by immigration attorneys before green card)

**California context:** Chinese and Indian communities are the largest and fastest-growing
wealthy immigrant populations in California. East West Bank (largest Chinese-American bank),
Cathay Bank, and international branches of HSBC all serve these communities and can be
referral sources.

### 5.3 Referral Network (Your Primary Channel)

Build relationships with professionals who already serve your target clients:

**Immigration attorneys:** Huge referral source. They see HNW clients at the immigration
decision point — exactly when pre-immigration planning is most valuable.

**International CPAs / tax attorneys:** Clients doing FBAR/FATCA filings need investment
guidance too. Offer to co-present or collaborate.

**US-licensed bankers at international banks:** HSBC Premier, Bank of China, East West
Bank, Cathay Bank — these banks serve immigrant HNW clients directly.

**Business attorneys handling cross-border transactions**

**Real estate agents in HNW immigrant communities**

**EB-5 regional centers** — Connect with firms processing investor visa applications

**How to build the network:**
- Offer free CE/seminar presentations to CPA and immigration law firms
- Write educational content (blog, LinkedIn) on pre-immigration planning, PFIC traps,
  estate tax for non-domiciliaries — establish yourself as the expert
- Join immigrant community professional associations

### 5.4 Community Presence

California has deep, organized immigrant professional communities:

- **Chinese/Taiwanese:** Committee of 100, Silicon Valley Chinese Association, Bay Area Council
- **Indian:** TiE (The Indus Entrepreneurs), SIPA (South Asian Professionals Association)
- **Korean:** Korean American Chamber of Commerce
- **General:** FPA (Financial Planning Association) local chapters, NAPFA
- **Language capability:** Mandarin, Cantonese, Hindi, Tamil fluency is a major asset

### 5.5 Content Marketing and Thought Leadership

Write or create content specifically for your niche. High-performing topics:
- "What happens to my foreign accounts when I get a green card?"
- "The PFIC trap: Why your foreign mutual funds become a tax nightmare in the US"
- "Estate tax planning for non-US citizens: The $60,000 exemption vs. $15 million trap"
- "Pre-immigration planning checklist for HNW investors"
- "FBAR vs FATCA: What every new US resident needs to know"

Publish on LinkedIn, and community-specific outlets. Speak at community events.
Consider content in Mandarin/Hindi/Korean for your target communities.

### 5.6 Fee Structure

For holistic planning with complex international clients:
- **Retainer / AUM fee:** 0.75%–1.25% of AUM (common for HNW clients)
- **Initial planning fee:** Flat fee for comprehensive cross-border plan ($5,000–$25,000
  depending on complexity — cross-border cases command a premium)
- **Ongoing retainer:** $500–$2,000/month for ongoing advisory access
- **Avoid commission-based:** Fiduciary RIA structure = fee-only or fee-based.
  This is a significant trust advantage over broker-dealers with wealthy immigrant clients.
- UHNWIs with $5M+ in assets often pay $25,000–$75,000+/year in retainer fees for
  comprehensive family wealth management

### 5.7 Compliance Considerations for Marketing

- All marketing materials must be reviewed for compliance with DFPI/SEC advertising rules
- Cannot guarantee investment results
- Testimonials are now permitted under SEC's updated marketing rule (2021) but require
  specific disclosures
- Document all client communications (compliance requirement)
- If advertising in a foreign language, those materials must also meet compliance standards

## CRITICAL WARNINGS AND COMMON PITFALLS

1. **Do not give investment advice before you are registered.** Holding yourself out as
   an investment adviser before completing Series 65 and RIA registration violates
   securities law. You can study and plan, but not practice.

2. **Tax advice has a separate legal lane.** Only CPAs, EAs, and attorneys can legally
   give specific tax advice for compensation. As a CFP without a CPA/EA license, be
   careful about what you call "tax advice" vs. "tax planning concepts."

3. **Do not custody client assets improperly.** Having custody triggers stricter
   requirements including surprise examinations by an independent CPA.

4. **FBAR/FATCA non-compliance is a tax matter.** Refer to a licensed tax professional
   (CPA/EA/attorney) for specific compliance filing work — you can identify the issue
   but may not be able to prepare the forms without the right credentials.

5. **AML obligations are evolving.** FinCEN is expanding AML requirements for RIAs.
   Wealthy immigrants with foreign assets may be subject to enhanced scrutiny. Have
   an AML awareness policy in place.

6. **California has stricter rules than many states.** California requires specific
   disclosures, financial requirements, and annual filings beyond what SEC-registered
   advisers must do.

7. **Avoid the PFIC trap for your own practice:** If you personally invest in foreign
   funds, you face the same PFIC issues as your clients. Model what you preach.
